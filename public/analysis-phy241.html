Trend Analysis

The Biggest Story: A Curriculum Overhaul in 2023

PHY 241 underwent a major scope change. The pre-2023 course was tightly focused on Electromagnetism and Optics. From 2023, the course became a general "Physics" course, adding mechanics, radiation science, environmental physics, and modern quantum physics. This means students cannot rely only on old papers.

Topics That Disappeared (2018–2022 → 2023+)

  • Kirchhoff's Laws — dominant for 4 years, absent in 2023–2024
  • AC Circuits (LCR, resonance, RMS) — 3 years, gone since 2022
  • Interference (Young's, Newton's rings) — 4 years, absent 2023–2024
  • Polarization (Brewster's, Malus's) — 4 years, absent 2023–2024
  • Radioactive Decay — 3 years, absent 2022–2024
  • Bohr's Atom / Hydrogen spectrum — 2 years, gone after 2020

Topics That Are Rising (2023–2024)

  • SHM / Mechanics — new in 2023, repeated in 2024
  • Blackbody Radiation — first in 2024 (high marks: ~7 marks)
  • Global Radiation & Solar Physics — first in 2024 (~8 marks)
  • Ecosystems & Environment — appeared in both 2023 & 2024
  • Quantum Mechanics — first in 2023 (wave function, Heisenberg)
  • X-ray Diffraction / Bragg's law — first in 2024

Repeated Questions Across Years

  • Biot-Savart for a long straight wire — appeared in 2020, 2022, and 2024. Very likely to continue.
  • Fresnel vs Fraunhofer diffraction distinction — appeared in 2018, 2019, 2020, 2022.
  • Self-inductance and mutual inductance definition + relation — appeared in 2020 and 2022 with identical wording.
  • Photoelectric effect + Einstein's equation — appeared in 2018, 2019, 2020 with near-identical wording.
  • de Broglie hypothesis — appeared in 2018, 2019, 2020, 2023.
  • Consecutive fringe distance in Young's double slit — appeared in 2018, 2019, 2020.
  • Brewster's law proof — appeared in 2018, 2019, 2020, 2022.

Consistent Structure Across All Years

Section A always covers electricity and magnetism. Section B covers optics and modern/applied physics. This split has held across all years. In 2023–2024, Section B shifted toward new physics topics while Section A kept the electromagnetism core.

Hidden Insights & Strategic Observations

  • Biot-Savart for a long wire is the most reliable derivation question: It has appeared in 2020, 2022, and 2024. In 2024, it was a standalone 4-mark sub-part. Know this derivation perfectly — it is free marks.
  • The Faraday/Lenz/EM Induction combination carries ~5–6 marks and appears 5 of 6 years: Any student who can state Faraday's law, explain Lenz's law with conservation of energy, and write an expression for mutual inductance covers two of the most reliable question areas.
  • Diffraction is the single highest-stakes Section B topic: The single-slit intensity derivation was worth 6 marks in 2022. Bragg's law appeared in 2024 for 3+1.5 marks. This is Section B's biggest mark opportunity in both old and new curriculum.
  • Ecosystem and Environmental questions are the easiest marks in 2023–2024: They are purely descriptive (no math), and each sub-part is only 1–1.5 marks. A student who reads the definitions can score 4–5 marks in 10 minutes.
  • Global Radiation (pyranometer) carries 5 marks in 2024: The largest single sub-part in Section B. It is purely descriptive. Students who prepare this topic can secure a large chunk of Section B marks quickly.
  • The AC Circuits (LCR, resonance, Q factor) block was extremely high-mark in 2018–2020 but disappeared after 2022. If the 2023–2024 trend continues, preparing AC circuits may be low ROI. However, if your exam follows the older syllabus, it becomes critical.
  • Coherent sources + fringe distance in Young's experiment appeared identically in 2018, 2019, and 2020: It's the most repeated optics sub-question and could easily return if the curriculum reverts to electromagnetism and optics.
  • The 2022 paper is the most important single reference because it's the most recent paper in the old "Electromagnetism and Optics" format. Topics from 2022 are more likely to return than topics from 2018–2019.

Prioritized Study Roadmap

First determine your syllabus version: If your 2024–2025 exam follows the new "Physics" curriculum, prepare SHM, Blackbody, Global Radiation, and Ecosystems as high priority. If it follows the older "Electromagnetism and Optics" curriculum, skip those and heavily prepare AC Circuits, Interference, and Polarization.

Phase 1 — Universal Core (Days 1–3): Present in ALL years

1
Gauss's Law & Electrostatics
Know the differential form derivation. Practice: electric field of spherical shell, long wire, dipole. Know Coulomb's law deduction from Gauss's law. Prepare 2–3 numericals on force/field calculation.
2
Biot-Savart Law — Derivation for a Long Straight Wire
This single derivation has appeared in 2020, 2022, 2024. Know it from memory (step-by-step, with diagram). Also know the circular conductor variation. Very high ROI — 4 marks in 30 minutes of preparation.
3
EM Induction + Self/Mutual Inductance
State Faraday's and Lenz's laws clearly. Know Lenz's law + conservation of energy argument. Derive mutual inductance for coaxial solenoids (appeared identically in 2022 and 2023). Prepare the energy stored in magnetic field formula.
4
Ampere's Law
State Ampere's law. Derive magnetic field for solenoid. Know the expression for field at axis of circular conductor. Prepare at least one numerical (B at a distance from wire).

Phase 2 — New Curriculum Priorities (Days 4–5): If your exam is 2023+ format

5
SHM — Show x = Acosωt + Bsinωt is solution + numerical
Know the differential equation of SHM and the verification proof. Practice calculating displacement, velocity, and acceleration from x = A cos(ωt + φ) at a given time. This is now a Section B staple.
6
Ecosystem & Environmental Physics
Pure memorization — no math. Prepare: ecosystem definition + examples, conservation of matter, environmental system components, food chain energy flow, greenhouse effect, ozone importance. 15–20 minutes of reading = 4+ marks.
7
Blackbody Radiation + Wien's Law numerical
Definitions of blackbody and blackbody radiation. Know the energy distribution curve sketch (3 temperatures, peak shifts). Know Wien's displacement law (λ_m T = constant) for the moon temperature calculation type. Know what albedo and earth's energy balance mean.
8
Global Radiation & Pyranometer
Know the definition of global radiation and factors affecting it. Describe the pyranometer method (5-mark question in 2024 — descriptive). Know albedo, earth energy balance concept.

Phase 3 — Optics Core (Days 5–6): High priority if old syllabus applies

9
Diffraction — Single slit intensity derivation + Bragg's law
The single-slit derivation was 6 marks in 2022. Know Fresnel vs Fraunhofer distinction. Know Bragg's law derivation and the lattice constant numerical. This is the Section B flagship topic.
10
Interference — Young's double slit + Newton's rings
Show that fringe spacing is λD/d (appears repeatedly). Know the Newton's rings radius formula and thin film condition (2μtcosr). Prepare the thin glass plate numerical.
11
Polarization — Brewster's law proof
State Brewster's law. Show reflected and refracted rays are at 90°. Know Malus's law. Know the quarter-wave plate thickness formula. Prepare one numerical.

Phase 4 — Modern Physics (Day 7)

12
Photoelectric Effect + de Broglie + Compton Effect
State Einstein's photoelectric equation. Know work function. State de Broglie hypothesis with formula. Know Compton effect shift formula. These appeared in 4 years and are quick 1–2 mark definitions + one numerical.
13
Radioactive Decay + Kirchhoff's Laws (if old syllabus)
Derive N = N₀e^(-λt). Know half-life and mean-life formulas and calculation. For Kirchhoff's: state KVL/KCL clearly, know the Wheatstone bridge derivation.

Exam Day Strategy

  • In Section A: always attempt the Gauss's law/electrostatics question first — it's the most predictable and structured
  • Biot-Savart derivation: if it appears, answer it immediately — it's almost guaranteed full marks
  • In Section B: if Ecosystem/Global Radiation appears, attempt it — it's purely descriptive and easy marks
  • For derivation sub-parts: draw a labeled diagram first — even if the derivation is incomplete, the diagram earns partial marks
  • Numericals: always write the formula → substitute values → calculate, even if the answer is wrong; the method earns marks
  • Don't skip the "Define X and Y" sub-parts (1–2 marks each) — they accumulate fast
