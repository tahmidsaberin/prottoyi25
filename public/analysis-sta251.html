Trend Analysis

Topics Rapidly Growing

  • Probability (all forms) — absent in 2017–2019, minor appearance in 2020–2022, then dominates 50%+ of the 2023 and 2024 papers. The most dramatic shift in STA 251 history.
  • Markov Chain / Stochastic Processes — appeared for the first time in 2024 paper. Will likely appear again in 2025.
  • Chebyshev's inequality / Markov inequality — introduced in 2024. A new skill required.
  • Computer science-flavored problems — from 2023 onward: code-based probability (randint), RTT of packets, process memory usage, algorithm execution times. Clearly tailored for CSE students.

Topics Stabilized or Declining

  • Introduction to Statistics / Data Collection — featured heavily in 2017–2021, now absent in 2023–2024. Likely dropped.
  • Skewness, Kurtosis, Moments — very prominent in 2017–2022, but absent in 2023–2024 papers entirely. May be de-emphasized going forward.
  • Multiple & Partial Correlation — appeared regularly in 2017–2022 but not in 2023–2024.
  • Correlation & Regression — stable across all years. These topics are permanent fixtures regardless of other changes.

The 2023–2024 Structural Shift

The 2023 paper (STA 251) is fundamentally different from 2017–2022. Section A is entirely probability-based — coin flips, drug tests, code probability, sample spaces — while Section B remains descriptive statistics. The 2024 paper goes further: Markov chains, Chebyshev bounds, binomial distributions, Bayes' theorem. This is not a gradual change; it is a curriculum overhaul. Prepare for both the old (descriptive) and new (probability) eras equally.

Repeated Questions Across Years (Exact or Near-Exact)

  • Correlation coefficient independence of origin and scale — proved in 2019, 2020, 2021. Very likely to appear again.
  • AM ≥ GM ≥ HM proof — appeared in 2019 and 2022. Classic theorem, always in scope.
  • Variance of first n natural numbers = (n²-1)/12 — appeared in 2020 and 2022. Likely to repeat.
  • Blood disease Binomial problem (p=0.4) — appeared with n=15 in 2021 Section A and n=100 in 2021 Section B (normal approximation). Highly probable repeat.
  • Spam filter problem (80% spam, 10% contain "free money") — appeared identically in both 2023 and 2024 papers. Almost certain to appear again.
  • randint-based code probability — appeared as a problem type in 2023 and 2024. Pattern is established.
  • Regression from partially destroyed records (equations given) — appeared in 2019 and similar in 2018. Classic problem type.

Hidden Insights & Strategic Observations

Non-Obvious High-Value Observations

  • The spam filter Bayes problem is essentially guaranteed. It appeared word-for-word in both 2023 and 2024. Solve it cold, from memory.
  • Regression is the most consistently marks-heavy topic. In almost every year there is one question worth 4–5 marks that involves fitting a regression line from raw data. This single question type can make or break your score.
  • The "blood disease" Binomial problem (p=0.4) is a recurring template. In 2021 it appeared twice in the same paper — once with n=15 (exact Binomial) and once with n=100 (normal approximation). Prepare both versions.
  • Correlation invariance proofs are asked almost every year. "Show that r is invariant to change of origin and scale" appeared in 2017, 2019, 2020, 2021. It will appear again. Memorize the proof.
  • The r²=b_xy·b_yx identity links regression and correlation. It appeared in 2018, 2019, and 2020. A four-step proof that bridges both topics.
  • Computer-flavored probability problems are a CSE-specific addition. RTT, RAM usage, code with randint — these did not exist in pre-2023 papers. They are now permanent. The practice problem set is mandatory preparation for 2025.
  • Multiple regression is medium-priority but high-marks. When it appears, it carries 3–5 marks and involves interpreting given correlation values (r₁₂, r₁₂.₃₄, β values). Learn the formula for partial correlation.

Common Traps

  • Confusing Pearson's r (interval data) with Spearman's ρ (rank data). Both appear, both have different formulas and interpretations.
  • Forgetting that there are two regression lines (y on x, and x on y), which are different unless r=±1. Many students only learn one.
  • In Binomial problems, "at least k survive" means P(X≥k) = 1 - P(X≤k-1). Check whether to use complementary counting.
  • The harmonic mean cannot be computed if any observation is zero — a classic one-mark trap question that appeared in 2022.

Topics Often Combined in One Question

  • Regression + Correlation (r²=b_xy·b_yx, or "prove r is geometric mean of both regression coefficients")
  • Moments + Skewness + Kurtosis (calculate all four moments, then compute β₁ and β₂)
  • Binomial Distribution + Normal Approximation (small n exact, large n approximate)
  • Conditional Probability + Bayes' Theorem (always combined in one problem)

Prioritized Study Roadmap

Phase 1 — Core Foundations (Days 1–3)

1
Correlation & Regression (master both together)
These two topics appear in every single paper and carry the most marks. Learn: Pearson's r formula, computation from raw data, invariance proof, scatter diagram interpretation. Regression: least squares derivation, two regression lines, r²=b_xy·b_yx proof, prediction. Practice at least 5 full numerical problems.
2
Central Tendency (AM, GM, HM, Median, Mode)
Learn all six formulas cold. Then master: AM≥GM≥HM proof, finding all measures from frequency distribution, trimmed mean concept, when to use which measure. Practice finding mean/median/mode/quartile/percentile from a grouped frequency table.
3
Measures of Dispersion (SD, Variance, CV, MD)
SD formula for grouped/ungrouped data. Proof that SD is independent of origin but not scale. Variance of n natural numbers = (n²-1)/12. CV = (SD/Mean)×100. When to prefer CV over SD. One large numerical problem from frequency distribution.

Phase 2 — Probability (Days 4–6)

4
Probability Basics + Bayes' Theorem
Start with axioms: P[A∪B] = P[A]+P[B]-P[A∩B]. Conditional probability definition. Independence vs pairwise independence vs mutual independence (the coin-flip problem). Then Bayes' theorem: spam filter problem by heart, machine defect problem, drug test problem. These appear repeatedly word-for-word.
5
Probability Distributions (Binomial, Poisson, Normal)
Binomial: PMF, mean=np, variance=npq. Compute P(X≥k) using complement. Normal approximation to Binomial. Poisson: λ=4, find P(X=6). PMF, PDF, CDF definitions. Joint distribution: E[X], E[Y], E[XY], covariance, marginal distributions.
6
Code-Based Probability + Markov Chains
randint(n) problems: understand modular arithmetic probability. P(num%3==0) = ⌊n/3⌋/n. Practice 2023 and 2024 code problems. For Markov Chain: draw transition diagram from matrix, define states, identify absorbing states. Know the definition of Stochastic Process, Birth-Death process.

Phase 3 — Secondary Topics (Days 7–8)

7
Skewness, Kurtosis & Moments
If 2025 follows the 2023–2024 pattern, these may be absent. But if it reverts to older format, they carry significant marks. Learn: definition of raw and central moments, relation between them. Karl Pearson's skewness coefficient. Bowley's coefficient. β₁ and β₂ formulas. Prove β₂ ≥ 1+β₁.
8
Multiple & Partial Correlation
Formula for R₁.₂₃ (multiple correlation). Formula for r₁₂.₃ (partial correlation). Given r₁₂, r₁₃, r₂₃ — compute multiple correlation, partial correlation, coefficient of determination. These are formula-heavy but all calculations are straightforward once you have the right formula.
9
Data Presentation, Introduction & Sampling
Quick revision only — these are low-priority in recent years. Know the difference between histogram (areas = frequencies) and bar diagram (heights = frequencies). Pie chart construction. Census vs sampling. Primary vs secondary data. Scales of measurement (nominal, ordinal, interval, ratio).
