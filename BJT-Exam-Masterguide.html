1. What is a BJT?

A Bipolar Junction Transistor (BJT) is a three-terminal semiconductor device that uses both electrons and holes (hence "bipolar") to control current flow. It can amplify signals or act as an electronic switch.

Key insight: A BJT is two PN junctions placed back-to-back, sharing a common middle region. The small current injected at the base controls a much larger current between collector and emitter.

2. Physical Structure

3. Why Each Region is Designed That Way

Emitter (E)

Heavily doped — maximum carrier injection. More doping = more electrons in NPN (or more holes in PNP) available for injection into base.

Base (B)

Very thin + lightly doped — minimizes recombination. Thin base = carriers injected by emitter reach collector before recombining.

Collector (C)

Large + moderately doped — collects carriers efficiently. Larger area = better heat dissipation. Moderately doped to handle high voltages.

⚠ Exam Trap
In PNP, conventional current flows in OPPOSITE direction compared to NPN. IB, IC, IE are all reversed. VBE is negative. Don't mix up polarities — this is the most common PNP mistake.

5. Charge Carriers & Doping Physics

Why "Bipolar"?

Both majority carriers (electrons in N-type, holes in P-type) and minority carriers participate in current conduction. This is what distinguishes BJTs from unipolar devices (FETs, which use only one carrier type).

  • N-type silicon: doped with phosphorus or arsenic → extra free electrons
  • P-type silicon: doped with boron or gallium → extra holes
  • At room temperature, VBE ≈ 0.7V for Si, 0.3V for Ge — this is the forward-bias threshold of the EB junction

6. The CORE Concept of BJT Action

Transistor action occurs because: The vast majority of carriers injected from the emitter do NOT recombine in the thin base region. Instead, they are swept directly into the collector by the reverse-bias electric field of the CB junction.

IC ≈ 98% of IE — only ~2% recombines in base to form IB. This is the fundamental mechanism behind current amplification.

Understand carrier movement step-by-step, all four operating regions, and exactly what happens at each junction under different bias conditions.

2. Step-by-Step Carrier Flow in NPN (Active Region)

1
Forward bias EBJ: VBE ≈ +0.7V applied. The barrier at EBJ is reduced. Electrons from the heavily-doped N-type emitter are injected across the junction into the P-type base. This injection creates IB (small) and the dominant flow toward collector.
2
Diffusion through thin base: Injected electrons enter the P-type base. A small fraction (~2%) recombine with holes in base → this recombination current is IB (supplied externally to replenish holes). The base is deliberately thin to minimize recombination.
3
Collection by reverse-biased CBJ: ~98% of injected electrons reach the CB junction. The reverse-bias electric field at the CB junction acts like a vacuum — it sweeps these electrons into the collector. This creates IC (large). No energy is needed; the field does the work.
4
Final result: A small IB (base current) controls a large IC (collector current). This is amplification. The ratio IC/IB = β (typically 50–300). IE = IC + IB flows out of the emitter.

3. Leakage Currents (From PDF — VERY IMPORTANT)

ICBO — Collector-Base Leakage

Exists even with IE = 0 (emitter open). Caused by thermally generated minority carriers in the base crossing the reverse-biased CB junction. Doubles roughly every 10°C rise in temperature.

ICEO — Collector-Emitter Leakage

Measured with IB = 0 (base open). This is the amplified version of ICBO. Because the transistor amplifies even leakage current:
ICEO = (1 + β) × ICBO

Full IC equation (with leakage)
IC = αIE + ICBO ← common-base form
IC = βIB + (1 + β)ICBO ← common-emitter form
ICEO = (1 + β)ICBO ← leakage amplification
⚠ Temperature Danger
ICBO doubles every 10°C. At high temperatures, ICEO can become significant enough to shift the Q-point, causing thermal runaway in poorly biased circuits.

4. PNP Operation (Differences from NPN)

In PNP, holes are the majority carriers injected from emitter. The bias polarities are reversed:

  • VEB is positive (emitter is more positive than base)
  • VEC is negative (collector is more negative than emitter)
  • Current directions: IB, IC flow out of the transistor (opposite to NPN convention)
  • Holes injected from P-emitter into N-base → same mechanism, opposite carrier type

1. Basic Current Relations

KCL at BJT — always true regardless of region
IE = IC + IB
IC = IE − IB
IB = IE − IC

2. Current Gains — α and β

Alpha (α) — Common Base Current Gain

α = IC / IE
Range: 0.95 ≤ α ≤ 0.998

Physical meaning: fraction of emitter current that reaches collector. Close to 1 because ~98% of carriers cross to collector.

Beta (β) — Common Emitter Current Gain

β = IC / IB
Typical range: 50 to 300

Physical meaning: amplification factor. A small IB controls a much larger IC. β is the key gain parameter in CE configuration.

3. α ↔ β Conversion (CRITICAL)

Converting between α and β — must memorize both directions
β = α / (1 − α)
α = β / (β + 1)
Example: α = 0.99 → β = 0.99/(1−0.99) = 0.99/0.01 = 99
Example: β = 100 → α = 100/101 = 0.9901

4. Current Expressions in Terms of α and β

Using α (Common Base)
IC = α · IE
IB = (1 − α) · IE
IE = IC / α
Using β (Common Emitter)
IC = β · IB
IE = (β + 1) · IB
IB = IC / β

5. With Leakage Current (Full Equations)

Include these when problem mentions ICBO or temperature effects
IC = α·IE + ICBO
IC = β·IB + (1 + β)·ICBO
ICEO = (1 + β) · ICBO ← leakage with base open

7. Dynamic (Small-Signal) Emitter Resistance re

AC resistance of the EB junction — very important for amplifier analysis
re = VT / IE
VT = 26 mV (at room temperature, T = 300K)
Example: IE = 2 mA → re = 26mV / 2mA = 13 Ω
💡 Derivation of VT
VT = kT/q where k = Boltzmann's constant (1.38×10⁻²³ J/K), T = 300K, q = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C. At room temp: (1.38×10⁻²³ × 300) / 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ ≈ 26 mV.

Essential voltage equations

9. Power Formula

Power dissipated in transistor
PC = VCE × IC
Must satisfy: PC < Pmax,   IC < IC(max),   VCE < BVCEO

10. Ebers-Moll Model (Conceptual Level)

The Ebers-Moll model is the complete mathematical model of a BJT valid in all regions. At exam level, the key equation is:

IC = IS(eVBE/VT − 1) − ICS(eVBC/VT − 1)

Where IS is saturation current (~10⁻¹⁵ A). In active region, VBC is large and negative, so second term ≈ 0, and IC ≈ IS·eVBE/VT. This explains the exponential IC vs VBE characteristic and why VBE is approximately constant (0.7V) over a wide range of IC.

11. Useful Derived Relations

Derived — useful for solving problems faster
IC = [α/(1−α)] · IB   = β · IB
IE = IC / α = (β+1)/β · IC
If α = 0.99 → β = 99;   If β = 49 → α = 49/50 = 0.98

1. Common Emitter (CE) — ⭐ Most Important

CE Key Facts

  • IC = β·IB — base current controls collector current with gain β
  • 180° phase inversion between input (base) and output (collector)
  • Highest power gain of all three configurations
  • Moderate input resistance → compatible with most signal sources
  • Used in virtually every analog amplifier stage in textbook problems

2. Common Base (CB)

CB Key Facts

  • Input: emitter. Output: collector. Base is common (AC ground).
  • Current gain < 1 (α ≈ 0.98) — no current amplification
  • Very high voltage gain and power gain due to high Ro/Ri ratio
  • No phase inversion (0°)
  • Very low input resistance → difficult to drive from voltage sources
  • Excellent high-frequency response → used in RF amplifiers, antenna stages
  • IC ≈ α·IE

3. Common Collector (CC) — Emitter Follower

CC Key Facts

  • Also called emitter follower — output follows input voltage
  • Voltage gain ≈ 1 (slightly less) — no voltage amplification
  • High current gain (β+1) and high power gain
  • Very high input resistance, very low output resistance
  • 0° phase shift
  • Primary use: impedance matching — buffers a high-impedance source to drive a low-impedance load
  • IE ≈ (β+1)·IB

4. Input/Output Characteristics of CE

Input Characteristics (IB vs VBE)

Similar to a forward-biased diode curve. IB rises exponentially once VBE exceeds ~0.5–0.7V. The curve shifts slightly right at higher VCE values. Key takeaway: IB is controlled by VBE (exponential relationship).

Output Characteristics (IC vs VCE)

Family of curves, each for a fixed IB. Three distinct regions:

  • Active: Flat, nearly horizontal lines (IC independent of VCE)
  • Saturation: Sharp rise at small VCE (< 0.2V)
  • Cutoff: Bottom curve, IB = 0, only ICEO flows

Biasing sets the DC operating point (Q-point). A poorly biased circuit will clip signals or drift with temperature. Master all three major techniques.

1. Why Bias a BJT?

The transistor must be in its active region to amplify. Biasing provides the DC voltages and currents that establish this operating point before any AC signal is applied. The Q-point (quiescent point) defines IC and VCE at rest.

2. Fixed Bias (Base Resistor Bias)

Circuit: RB from VCC to Base, RC from VCC to Collector

Analysis equations
IB = (VCC − VBE) / RB
IC = β · IB   (active region)
VCE = VCC − IC · RC
⚠ Major Problem
IC = β·IB means IC depends directly on β. Since β varies ±50% between transistors of same type, and β changes with temperature, the Q-point is highly unstable. Fixed bias is simple but almost never used in practice.

3. Collector-to-Base Bias (Feedback Bias)

Circuit: RB connected from Collector to Base (not VCC)

Analysis equations
IB = (VCC − VBE) / (RB + (β+1)·RC)
IC = β · IB
VCE = VCC − (IC + IB)·RC ≈ VCC − IC·RC

Stability improvement: If IC tries to increase (e.g., β rises with temperature), VCE drops, which reduces voltage across RB, reducing IB, which partially counteracts IC rise. This is negative feedback.

Better than fixed bias, but Q-point still depends significantly on β.

4. Voltage Divider Bias (Most Stable — Most Important)

Circuit: R1 and R2 form a voltage divider; RE in emitter

Step-by-step analysis (Thevenin equivalent)
VTH = VCC × R2 / (R1 + R2)   ← Thevenin voltage at base
RTH = R1 ∥ R2   ← Thevenin resistance
IB = (VTH − VBE) / (RTH + (β+1)·RE)
IC = β · IB
VCE = VCC − IC(RC + RE)

Why it's stable: RE creates negative feedback via emitter degeneration. If IC rises, VE = IC·RE rises, VBE = VB − VE falls (since VB is fixed by divider), IB drops, IC drops back. This automatic correction makes Q-point nearly independent of β.

Condition for stability: When β·RE ≫ RTH, the Q-point becomes almost entirely independent of β.

5. Stability Comparison

6. Thermal Runaway

⚠ Critical Concept
Temperature ↑ → ICBO ↑ → IC ↑ → Power dissipation ↑ → Temperature ↑ (cycle repeats). Without proper biasing (RE stabilization or heat sink), this positive feedback loop can destroy the transistor. RE is the primary defense.

7. Saturation Check

💡 How to check if transistor is in active or saturated region

1. Assume active region: calculate IC = β·IB
2. Calculate VCE = VCC − IC·RC
3. If VCE > VCE(sat) ≈ 0.2V → active region ✔
4. If VCE ≤ 0.2V → transistor is saturated. Use VCE = 0.2V and recalculate IC from KVL.

2. Load Line Analysis

DC Load Line Equation

VCE = VCC − IC · RC   (for simple CE with RC only)
VCE = VCC − IC · (RC + RE)   (with emitter resistor)

The load line is a straight line with two key points:

  • Y-intercept (IC-axis): Set VCE = 0 → IC(max) = VCC / RC — saturation point
  • X-intercept (VCE-axis): Set IC = 0 → VCE = VCC — cutoff point
  • Q-point: Intersection of load line with the characteristic curve corresponding to the DC bias IB

3. How to Solve Graph Questions (Step-by-Step)

1
Identify which IB curve the Q-point lies on (or calculate it from biasing circuit)
2
Draw the load line: connect IC(max) = VCC/RC on y-axis to VCE = VCC on x-axis
3
Find Q-point = intersection of load line with the IB curve from step 1
4
Read ICQ and VCEQ from graph. Verify VCEQ > 0.2V (active region)
5
For AC analysis: AC load line passes through Q-point with slope = −1/(RC ∥ RL)

Two primary applications: small-signal amplifier and digital switch. Know both analytically.

1. BJT as Amplifier (Small-Signal Analysis)

The re Model (Simplified)

For small AC signals around the Q-point, the transistor is replaced by its small-signal equivalent:

Voltage gain: AV = −β·RC / (β·re) = −RC / re
Or: AV ≈ −RC / re   where re = 26mV / IE
Current gain: AI = IC / IB = β
Power gain: AP = AV × AI = (RC/re) × β
Input resistance: Ri = β · re

The minus sign in AV confirms 180° phase inversion for CE configuration.

💡 Example calculation
Given: VCC=12V, RC=4kΩ, RE=1kΩ, R1=47kΩ, R2=10kΩ, β=100
VB = 12 × 10/(47+10) = 2.1V | VE = 2.1 − 0.7 = 1.4V | IE = 1.4V/1kΩ = 1.4mA
re = 26mV/1.4mA = 18.6Ω | AV = −4000/18.6 ≈ −215 (gain of 215, inverted)

2. BJT as Switch

Switch OFF — Cutoff

Condition: VBE < 0.5V (below threshold)
Result: IB = 0, IC ≈ 0
VCE ≈ VCC (high, appears as open circuit)
Digital equivalent: Logic 0 at input → open switch

Switch ON — Saturation

Condition: IB ≥ IC(sat)/β (overdrive)
Result: VCE(sat) ≈ 0.2V
IC = (VCC − VCE(sat)) / RC ≈ VCC / RC
Digital equivalent: Logic 1 at input → closed switch

Switch design equations
IC(sat) = (VCC − VCE(sat)) / RC ≈ VCC / RC
IB(min) = IC(sat) / β   ← minimum base current to saturate
RB = (Vin − VBE) / IB   ← design base resistor
For reliable saturation: IB > IC(sat)/β (use 2-3× to ensure saturation)

3. BJT in Digital Logic

Input (Vin)BJT RegionOutput (Vout = VCE)Logic Level
Low (0V)Cutoff≈ VCC (high)Logic 1
High (VCC)Saturation≈ 0.2V (low)Logic 0

This is a NOT gate (inverter) — BJT CE inverts the logic level. This 180° inversion is the basis of RTL (Resistor-Transistor Logic) and TTL logic families.

4. Other Applications

  • Current mirror: Two matched BJTs share VBE → IC2 mirrors IC1
  • Darlington pair: Two BJTs in cascade, total β = β1 × β2 (ultra-high gain)
  • Oscillators: CE amplifier with positive feedback at 180° phase shift
  • Push-pull amplifier: NPN + PNP in CC configuration for power amplification
  • Temperature sensor: VBE varies −2mV/°C (predictable, usable)

Common Exam Mistakes (Directly from PDF + Extended)

❌ Mistake 1: Ignoring ICBO when it's explicitly given
If a problem gives ICBO, you MUST use IC = βIB + (1+β)ICBO. Using IC = βIB alone will give wrong answer. ICEO also requires the full formula.
❌ Mistake 2: Mixing up α and β formulas
α = IC/IE (not IB/IE). β = IC/IB (not IE/IB). The confusion is: both look like "current ratio." Remember: α uses IE (the big current), β uses IB (the small current).
❌ Mistake 3: Wrong current direction in PNP
In PNP: holes flow from emitter to base. Conventional current directions are opposite to NPN. VBE is negative, VCE is negative. Don't apply NPN polarity rules to PNP circuits.
❌ Mistake 4: Not checking which region (cutoff/saturation/active)
Always verify: if β·IB > IC(sat), the transistor is in SATURATION — use VCE = 0.2V, not VCE = VCC − IC·RC. Assuming active region blindly is a very common error.
❌ Mistake 5: Confusing βac and βdc
βdc = IC/IB (absolute values). βac = ΔIC/ΔIB (incremental, from graph slope). In most problems they're assumed equal unless told otherwise.
❌ Mistake 6: Wrong α↔β conversion
β = α/(1−α) not α/(α−1). α = β/(β+1) not β/(β−1). Check: if β=100, α should be close to 1. If your α > 1, you made an error.
❌ Mistake 7: Forgetting that IC is nearly independent of VCE (active region)
In the active region, IC is controlled only by IB (or IE), not by VCE. Graph questions often test this. IC stays flat as VCE changes in active region.
❌ Mistake 8: Using wrong VBE value
Silicon: VBE = 0.7V. Germanium: VBE = 0.3V. If the problem specifies material, use correct value. If unspecified and it's a modern circuit, default to 0.7V (Si).
❌ Mistake 9: Not applying Thevenin theorem to voltage divider bias
Always Thevenize R1-R2 divider before writing KVL for the base loop. Writing VCC−R1·IB−VBE−RE·IE = 0 (treating VCC as if directly connected) misses the interaction between R1 and R2.
❌ Mistake 10: re formula confusion
re = VT/IE = 26mV/IE. Some books write re = 26/IE(mA) in Ohms. Make sure units match: IE in amperes → re in ohms directly from 0.026/IE(A). Or IE in mA → re = 26/IE(mA) ohms.

Conceptual Trick Questions

Q: "If VBE increases from 0.65V to 0.70V, what happens to IC?"

IC increases exponentially (Ebers-Moll), approximately doubling for every 18mV increase at room temperature. A 50mV increase can multiply IC by ~10×. This is NOT linear.

Q: "Why does IC stay approximately constant as VCE varies in active region?"

Because the CB junction is reverse-biased in active region. More reverse bias just sweeps carriers faster — but the supply of carriers is fixed by IB and α. Only minority carriers (ICBO) depend on VCE magnitude.

Q: "In saturation, does IC = β·IB?"

NO. In saturation, the normal amplifier relationship breaks down. IC < β·IB. VCE is clamped at ~0.2V. Calculate IC from VCE = 0.2V using KVL, not from β·IB.

Problem 1 — Basic α and β Calculation

Easy

A transistor has IE = 10 mA and IB = 0.2 mA. Find IC, α, and β.

Given: IE = 10 mA, IB = 0.2 mA

Step 1: Find IC using KCL
IE = IC + IB → IC = IE − IB = 10 − 0.2 = 9.8 mA

Step 2: Find α
α = IC / IE = 9.8 / 10 = 0.98

Step 3: Find β
β = IC / IB = 9.8 / 0.2 = 49

Verification: β = α/(1−α) = 0.98/0.02 = 49 ✔

IC = 9.8 mA | α = 0.98 | β = 49

Problem 2 — Given α, Find β and Currents

Easy

For a transistor with α = 0.99 and IE = 5 mA, find β, IC, and IB.

Given: α = 0.99, IE = 5 mA

Step 1: Find β
β = α/(1−α) = 0.99/(1−0.99) = 0.99/0.01 = 99

Step 2: Find IC
IC = α·IE = 0.99 × 5 = 4.95 mA

Step 3: Find IB
IB = IE − IC = 5 − 4.95 = 0.05 mA = 50 µA

Check: β = IC/IB = 4.95/0.05 = 99 ✔

β = 99 | IC = 4.95 mA | IB = 50 µA

Problem 3 — Fixed Bias Q-Point

Medium

CE circuit: VCC = 12V, RC = 2.2 kΩ, RB = 470 kΩ, β = 80, VBE = 0.7V. Find ICQ and VCEQ. Check if in active region.

VCC=12V, RC=2.2kΩ, RB=470kΩ, β=80, VBE=0.7V

Step 1: Find IB
IB = (VCC − VBE) / RB = (12 − 0.7) / 470000 = 11.3 / 470000 = 24.04 µA

Step 2: Assume active region, find IC
IC = β·IB = 80 × 24.04µA = 1.923 mA

Step 3: Find VCE
VCE = VCC − IC·RC = 12 − (1.923×10⁻³)(2200) = 12 − 4.23 = 7.77 V

Step 4: Verify active region
VCE = 7.77V > 0.2V ✔ → Active region confirmed

ICQ ≈ 1.92 mA | VCEQ ≈ 7.77 V | Active region ✔

Problem 4 — With Leakage Current ICBO

Medium

A transistor has β = 50, IB = 40 µA, ICBO = 2 µA. Find actual IC and ICEO.

β = 50, IB = 40 µA, ICBO = 2 µA

Step 1: Full IC formula (with leakage)
IC = β·IB + (1+β)·ICBO = 50×40µA + (51)×2µA = 2000µA + 102µA = 2.102 mA

Step 2: Without leakage (approximate)
IC ≈ β·IB = 50×40µA = 2.0 mA

Step 3: Error from ignoring ICBO
Error = (2.102−2.0)/2.102 × 100% ≈ 4.9% — significant when ICBO is large!

Step 4: Find ICEO
ICEO = (1+β)·ICBO = (1+50)×2µA = 51×2µA = 102 µA

IC = 2.102 mA | ICEO = 102 µA

Problem 5 — Voltage Divider Bias (Full Analysis)

Hard

CE with VD bias: VCC=15V, R1=39kΩ, R2=10kΩ, RC=3.9kΩ, RE=1kΩ, β=100, VBE=0.7V. Find Q-point (ICQ, VCEQ).

VCC=15V, R1=39kΩ, R2=10kΩ, RC=3.9kΩ, RE=1kΩ, β=100

Step 1: Thevenin equivalent at base
VTH = VCC × R2/(R1+R2) = 15 × 10/(39+10) = 15 × 10/49 = 3.06 V

RTH = R1∥R2 = (39×10)/(39+10) = 390/49 = 7.96 kΩ

Step 2: Write KVL for base loop
VTH = IB·RTH + VBE + IE·RE
Since IE ≈ (β+1)·IB = 101·IB:
3.06 = IB×7960 + 0.7 + 101×IB×1000
2.36 = IB×(7960 + 101000) = IB × 108960
IB = 2.36 / 108960 = 21.66 µA

Step 3: Find IC and VCE
IC = β·IB = 100 × 21.66µA = 2.166 mA
VE = IE·RE ≈ IC·RE = 2.166mA × 1kΩ = 2.166V
VCE = VCC − IC(RC+RE) = 15 − 2.166mA×(3.9k+1k) = 15 − 10.61 = 4.39 V

Step 4: Verify: VCE = 4.39V > 0.2V → Active ✔

ICQ ≈ 2.17 mA | VCEQ ≈ 4.39 V | Active region ✔

Problem 6 — Saturation Check

Hard

CE circuit: VCC=5V, RC=1kΩ, RB=10kΩ, β=100, VBE=0.7V. Is transistor in active or saturation?

VCC=5V, RC=1kΩ, RB=10kΩ, β=100

Step 1: Assume active, find IB
IB = (5−0.7)/10000 = 4.3/10000 = 0.43 mA = 430 µA

Step 2: Calculate IC assuming active
IC = β·IB = 100 × 430µA = 43 mA

Step 3: Calculate VCE with this IC
VCE = 5 − 43mA × 1kΩ = 5 − 43 = −38V ← IMPOSSIBLE!

Conclusion: Transistor IS in saturation. VCE cannot be negative.

Step 4: Saturation analysis
VCE(sat) = 0.2V
IC(sat) = (VCC − VCE(sat))/RC = (5−0.2)/1000 = 4.8 mA
IB(sat needed) = IC(sat)/β = 4.8mA/100 = 48µA
Actual IB = 430µA ≫ 48µA → Deeply saturated ✔

Problem 7 — Small Signal Amplifier Gain

Hard

CE amplifier: IC(Q) = 2mA, RC = 5kΩ. Find re, voltage gain AV, and input resistance Ri if β=120.

ICQ = 2mA, RC = 5kΩ, β = 120

Step 1: Find IE ≈ IC (approximation)
IE ≈ IC = 2 mA (since IB ≪ IC)

Step 2: Find re
re = VT / IE = 26mV / 2mA = 13 Ω

Step 3: Voltage gain
AV = −RC / re = −5000 / 13 = −384.6 ≈ −385
(Negative sign = 180° phase inversion)

Step 4: Input resistance
Ri = β × re = 120 × 13 = 1560 Ω = 1.56 kΩ

Memory Shortcuts

  • α → Alpha uses IE (the All-current: biggest one)
  • β → Beta uses IB (the Base current: smallest one)
  • α close to 1 → collector gets almost ALL of emitter current
  • VBE = 0.7V Si → "Seven for Silicon"
  • ICEO = (1+β)×ICBO → leakage is AMPLIFIED by (1+β)
  • CE phase = 180° → CE "Changes sign" (inverts)
  • CB no phase shift → "CB is Calm" (no inversion)
  • CC voltage gain ≈ 1 → "CC Copies voltage" (follower)
  • re = 26/IE(mA) Ω → "26 over IE in milliamps"
  • Saturation: VCE ≈ 0.2V → "barely two-tenths above ground"
